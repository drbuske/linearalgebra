    Section 5.2 Linear combinations, span, and linear independence

    In this section, we will again explore concepts introduced earlier in terms of \(\mathbb{R}^n\) and extend them to apply to abstract vector spaces. We can now revisit many of the concepts earlier introduced in the context of general vector spaces.
    🔗

    Preview Activity 5.2.1.

    In the previous section, we learned what it means for a set of objects to form a vector space. In this section, we ask a new question: how can vectors be built from other vectors?
    🔗
    Rather than beginning with formal definitions, this activity invites you to experiment with combinations of vectors and to identify patterns that will later be captured by precise mathematical language.
    🔗

    (a)

    Consider the vectors \(\uvec=\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\vvec=\begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. List at least five different vectors that can be formed using expressions of the form \(a\uvec+b\vvec\text{,}\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are real numbers.
      🔗
    2. Describe, in words, the collection of all vectors that can be formed this way.
      🔗
    3. What changes if you restrict \(a\) and \(b\) to be integers?
      🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Now consider the vectors \(\wvec=\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}\) and \(\zvec=\begin{bmatrix}2 \\ 2\end{bmatrix}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Which vectors can be formed using expressions of the form \(a\wvec+b\zvec\text{?}\)
      🔗
    2. Compare your answer with the result from the previous task.
      🔗
    3. What seems different about the roles played by \(\wvec\) and \(\zvec\) compared to \(\uvec\) and \(\vvec\text{?}\)
      🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Consider the set of polynomials \(p_1(x)=x\) and \(p_2(x)=x^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Write several expressions that combine \(p_1\) and \(p_2\) using multiplication by numbers and addition.
      🔗
    2. Which polynomials can be created this way?
      🔗
    3. Is it possible to create the polynomial \(1\) using these two polynomials? Explain.
      🔗
    🔗
    This activity suggests that some collections of vectors are powerful enough to generate large portions of a vector space, while others are limited or redundant. In the remainder of this section, we will develop precise language to describe these ideas using the concepts of linear combinations, span, and linear independence.
    🔗
    🔗
    We will look at linear combinations, span, and linear independence in this section, and at subspaces, bases, and dimension in the next section.

    Subsection 5.2.1 Linear combinations

    Definition 5.2.1.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space over a field \(K\text{.}\) Let \(\uvec_1,\ldots,\uvec_n \in V\text{.}\) A vector \(\vvec \in V\) is called a linear combination of \(\uvec_1,\ldots,\uvec_n\) if there exist scalars \(a_1,\ldots,a_n \in K\) such that
    \begin{gather*} \vvec = a_1\uvec_1 + \cdots + a_n\uvec_n. \end{gather*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.2.2.

    We write the matrix
    \begin{equation*} A=\begin{bmatrix}1\amp3\\-1\amp2\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    as a linear combination of
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}1\amp 0\\0\amp 1\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1\amp 0\\0\amp -1\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\amp 1\\1\amp 0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\amp -1\\1\amp 0\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We must find coefficients \(a,b,c,d\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}1\amp 3\\-1\amp 2\end{bmatrix} = a\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 0\\0\amp 1\end{bmatrix} +b\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 0\\0\amp -1\end{bmatrix} +c\begin{bmatrix}0\amp 1\\1\amp 0\end{bmatrix} +d\begin{bmatrix}0\amp -1\\1\amp 0\end{bmatrix}, \end{equation*}
    or equivalently,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}1\amp 3\\-1\amp 2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a+b \amp c-d \\ c+d \amp a-b \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This yields a system of four equations in four variables:
    \begin{align*} a+b \amp = 1, \\ c+d \amp = -1, \\ c-d \amp = 3, \\ a-b \amp = 2. \end{align*}
    We can easily solve the system of equations to find the unique solution \(a=\tfrac32\text{,}\) \(b=-\tfrac12\text{,}\) \(c=1\text{,}\) \(d=-2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Therefore
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}1\amp3\\-1\amp2\end{bmatrix} = \tfrac32\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 0\\0\amp 1\end{bmatrix} -\tfrac12\begin{bmatrix}1\amp 0\\0\amp -1\end{bmatrix} +1\begin{bmatrix}0\amp 1\\1\amp 0\end{bmatrix} -2\begin{bmatrix}0\amp -1\\1\amp 0\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.2.3.

    We write the polynomial \(p(x)=7x^2+4x-3\) as a linear combination of \(q_1(x)=x^2\text{,}\) \(q_2(x)=(x+1)^2\text{,}\) and \(q_3(x)=(x+2)^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Note that \(q_2(x)=(x+1)^2=x^2+2x+1\) and \(q_3(x)=(x+2)^2=x^2+4x+4\text{.}\) We must find coefficients \(a,b,c\) such that
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=a\cdot q_1(x)+b\cdot q_2(x)+c\cdot q_3(x), \end{equation*}
    or equivalently,
    \begin{equation*} 7x^2+4x-3=ax^2+b(x^2+2x+1)+c(x^2+4x+4). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Collecting equal powers of \(x\text{,}\) we can rewrite this as
    \begin{equation*} 7x^2+4x-3=(a+b+c)x^2+(2b+4c)x+(b+4c). \end{equation*}
    Since two polynomials are equal if and only if each corresponding coefficient is equal, this yields a system of three equations in three variables:
    \begin{gather*} a+b+c=7,\\ 2b+4c=4,\\ b+4c=-3. \end{gather*}
    🔗
    We can easily solve this system of equations and find that the unique solution is \(a=\tfrac52\text{,}\) \(b=7\text{,}\) and \(c=-\tfrac52\text{.}\) Therefore
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=\tfrac52q_1(x)+7q_2(x)-\tfrac52q_3(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 5.2.2 Span of a set of vectors

    As earlier seen, the span of a set of vectors is defined as the set of all of its linear combinations. We generalize the concept of span to consider spans of arbitrary (possibly finite, possibly infinite) sets of vectors.
    🔗

    Definition 5.2.4.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space over some field \(K\text{,}\) and let \(S\) be a set of vectors (i.e., a subset of \(V\)). The span of \(S\) is the set of all linear combinations of elements of \(S\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In symbols,
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{span}(S) = \{a_1\uvec_1+\cdots+a_k\uvec_k \mid \uvec_1,\ldots,\uvec_k\in S,\ a_1,\ldots,a_k\in K\}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    It is important not to misunderstand this definition. Even when the set \(S\) is infinite, each individual element \(\vvec \in\mathrm{span}(S)\) is a linear combination of only finitely many elements \(\uvec_1,\ldots,\uvec_k\) of \(S\text{.}\) The definition does not talk about infinite linear combinations \(a_1\uvec_1+a_2\uvec_2+a_3\uvec_3+\cdots\text{.}\) Indeed, such infinite sums do not typically exist.
    🔗
    However, different elements \(\vvec,\wvec\in\mathrm{span}(S)\) can be linear combinations of a different (finite) number of vectors of \(S\text{.}\) For example, it is possible that \(\vvec\) is a linear combination of 10 elements of \(S\text{,}\) and \(\wvec\) is a linear combination of 100 elements of \(S\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 5.2.5.

    Consider the vector space \(\mathrm{Seq}_{\mathbb{R}}\) of infinite sequences of real numbers. For every \(k\in\mathbb{N}\text{,}\) let \(e^k\) be the sequence whose \(k\)th element is 1 and that is 0 everywhere else, i.e.,
    \begin{gather*} e^0=(1,0,0,0,0,\ldots),\\ e^1=(0,1,0,0,0,\ldots),\\ e^2=(0,0,1,0,0,\ldots), \end{gather*}
    and so on. Let \(S=\{e^k : k\in\mathbb{N}\}\text{.}\) Which of the following sequences are in \(\mathrm{span}(S)\text{?}\)
    🔗
    \begin{gather*} \text{(a)}\ f=(1,1,1,0,0,0,0,0,\ldots), \\ \text{(b)}\ g=(1,2,0,5,0,0,0,0,\ldots),\\ \text{(c)}\ h=(1,1,1,1,1,1,1,1,\ldots), \\ \text{(d)}\ m=(1,0,1,0,1,0,1,0,\ldots). \end{gather*}
    🔗
    (a) We have \(f\in\mathrm{span}(S)\text{,}\) because \(f=e^0+e^1+e^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (b) We have \(g\in\mathrm{span}(S)\text{,}\) because \(g=1e^0+2e^1+5e^3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    (c) The sequence \(h\) is not in \(\mathrm{span}(S)\text{,}\) because each element of \(\mathrm{span}(S)\) is, by definition, a linear combination of finitely many elements of \(S\text{.}\) No linear combinations of finitely many \(e^k\) can end in infinitely many ones. Note that we are not permitted to write an infinite sum such as \(e^0+e^1+e^2+\cdots\text{.}\) Such infinite sums are not defined in vector spaces.
    🔗
    (d) The sequence \(m\) is not in \(\mathrm{span}(S)\text{,}\) for the same reason. We would need to add infinitely many sequences of the form \(e^k\) to get a sequence that contains infinitely many non-zero elements. However, this is not permitted by the definition of span.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.2.6.

    Let \(p(x)=7x^2+4x-3\text{.}\) Is \(p(x)\in\mathrm{span}\{x^2,(x+1)^2,(x+2)^2\}\text{?}\)
    🔗
    The answer is yes, because we found in Example 5.2.3 that
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=\tfrac52x^2+7(x+1)^2-\tfrac52(x+2)^2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    We say that a set of vectors \(S\) is a spanning set for \(V\) if \(V=\mathrm{span}(S)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 5.2.7.

    Let \(S=\{x^2,(x+1)^2,(x+2)^2\}\text{.}\) We show that \(S\) is a spanning set for \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{,}\) the vector space of all polynomials of degree at most 2.
    🔗
    This is analogous to Example 5.2.3. Consider an arbitrary element \(p(x)=p_2x^2+p_1x+p_0\) of \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{.}\) We must show that \(p(x)\in\mathrm{span}(S)\text{,}\) i.e., that there exist \(a,b,c\in \mathbb{R}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=ax^2+b(x+1)^2+c(x+2)^2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We can equivalently rewrite this equation as
    \begin{equation*} p_2x^2+p_1x+p_0=(a+b+c)x^2+(2b+4c)x+(b+4c), \end{equation*}
    which yields the system of equations
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} a + b + c \amp = p_2 \\ 2b + 4c \amp = p_1 \\ b + 4c \amp = p_0 \end{aligned} \qquad \simeq \qquad \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp p_2 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 4 \amp p_1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 4 \amp p_0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \simeq \qquad \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp p_2 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 4 \amp p_1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 4 \amp 2p_0 - p_1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since the system has rank 3, it has a solution. Therefore \(p(x)\in\mathrm{span}(S)\text{.}\) Since \(p(x)\) was an arbitrary element of \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{,}\) it follows that \(S\) is a spanning set for \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 5.2.3 Linear independence

    We now define the concept of linear independence in a general vector space. Here too, we generalize the definition to an arbitrary (finite or infinite) set of vectors.
    🔗

    Definition 5.2.8.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space over a field \(K\text{.}\) A finite set of vectors \(\{\uvec_1,\ldots,\uvec_k\}\) is called linearly independent if the equation
    \begin{equation*} a_1\uvec_1+\cdots+a_k\uvec_k=0 \end{equation*}
    has only the trivial solution
    \begin{equation*} a_1=\cdots=a_k=0. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    An infinite set of vectors is linearly independent if every finite subset is linearly independent.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.2.9.

    Determine whether the polynomials \(x^2\text{,}\) \(x^2+2x-1\text{,}\) and \(2x^2-x+3\) are linearly independent.
    🔗
    According to the definition of linear independence, we must solve the equation
    \begin{equation*} ax^2+b(x^2+2x-1)+c(2x^2-x+3)=0. \end{equation*}
    If there is a non-trivial solution, the polynomials are linearly dependent. If there is only the trivial solution, they are linearly independent.
    🔗
    We first rearrange the left-hand side to collect equal powers of \(x\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} (a+b+2c)x^2+(2b-c)x+(3c-b)=0. \end{equation*}
    This turns into a system of 3 equations in 3 variables:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} a+b+2c \amp = 0 \\ 2b-c \amp = 0 \\ 3c-b \amp = 0 \end{aligned} \simeq \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -1 \amp 3 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \simeq \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp -1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since the system has rank 3, there are no free variables. The only solution is \(a=b=c=0\text{,}\) and the polynomials are linearly independent.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.2.10.

    Consider again the real-valued sequences from Example 5.2.5,
    \begin{gather*} e^0=(1,0,0,0,0,\ldots),\\ e^1=(0,1,0,0,0,\ldots),\\ e^2=(0,0,1,0,0,\ldots), \end{gather*}
    and so on. Let \(S=\{e^0,e^1,e^2,\ldots\}\text{.}\) This is an infinite subset of \(\mathrm{Seq}_{\mathbb{R}}\text{.}\) We show that \(S\) is linearly independent.
    🔗
    Since \(S\) is an infinite set, we have to show that every finite subset of \(S\) is linearly independent. So consider a finite subset
    \begin{equation*} \{e^{k_1},e^{k_2},\ldots,e^{k_n}\}\subseteq S \end{equation*}
    and assume that
    \begin{equation*} a_1e^{k_1}+a_2e^{k_2}+\cdots+a_ne^{k_n}=0. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We have to show that \(a_1,\ldots,a_n=0\text{.}\) Consider some index \(i\in\{1,\ldots,n\}\text{.}\) Then the \(k_i\)th element of \(a_1e^{k_1}+\cdots+a_ne^{k_n}\) is equal to \(a_i\) by the left-hand side of the equation, but it is also equal to 0 by the right-hand side. It follows that \(a_i=0\) for all \(i\in\{1,\ldots,n\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Therefore \(e^{k_1},e^{k_2},\ldots,e^{k_n}\) is linearly independent. Since this was an arbitrary finite subset of \(S\text{,}\) it follows, by definition, that \(S\) is linearly independent.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.2.11.

    We determine whether the following elements of \(\Mvec_{2,2}\) are linearly independent:
    \begin{equation*} M_1=\begin{bmatrix}-1 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp -1\end{bmatrix},\quad M_2=\begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp 2\end{bmatrix},\quad M_3=\begin{bmatrix}1 \amp 3 \\ 5 \amp 4\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We look for solutions to \(aM_1+bM_2+cM_3=0\text{.}\) Notice that this equation has non-trivial solutions, for example \(a=2\text{,}\) \(b=3\text{,}\) and \(c=-1\text{.}\) Therefore the matrices are linearly dependent.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.2.12. Linearly independent functions.

    In the vector space \(\mathrm{Func}_{\mathbb{R},\mathbb{R}}\) of real-valued functions on the real numbers, we show that the functions \(f(x)=\sin x\) and \(g(x)=\cos x\) are linearly independent.
    🔗
    Assume \(A\sin x+B\cos x=0\text{.}\) Note that this is an equality of functions, which means that it is true for all \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In particular, substituting \(x=0\) into the equation, and using the fact that \(\sin 0=0\) and \(\cos 0=1\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} 0=A\sin 0+B\cos 0=A\cdot 0+B\cdot 1=B, \end{equation*}
    and therefore \(B=0\text{.}\) On the other hand, substituting \(x=\tfrac{\pi}{2}\) into the equation, and using the fact that \(\sin\tfrac{\pi}{2}=1\) and \(\cos\tfrac{\pi}{2}=0\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} 0=A\sin\tfrac{\pi}{2}+B\cos\tfrac{\pi}{2} =A\cdot 1+B\cdot 0=A, \end{equation*}
    and therefore \(A=0\text{.}\) Therefore the equation \(A\sin x+B\cos x=0\) only has the trivial solution \(A=B=0\text{,}\) and it follows that \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\) are linearly independent.
    🔗
    🔗
    The properties of linear independence that were discussed earlier remain true in the general setting of vector spaces. We also have the usual characterization of linear dependence in terms of redundant vectors:
    🔗

    Proposition 5.2.13.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space, and let \(\uvec_1,\uvec_2,\ldots\) be a (finite or infinite) sequence of vectors in \(V\text{.}\) If \(\uvec_1,\uvec_2,\ldots\) are linearly dependent, then at least one of the vectors can be written as a linear combination of earlier vectors in the sequence:
    \begin{equation*} \uvec_j = a_1\uvec_1 + a_2\uvec_2 + \cdots + a_{j-1}\uvec_{j-1}, \end{equation*}
    for some \(j\text{.}\) We say that the vector \(\uvec_j\) is redundant.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose that the vectors are linearly dependent. Then the equation \(b_1\uvec_1 + \cdots + b_k\uvec_k = 0\) has a non-trivial solution for some \(k\text{.}\) In other words, there exist scalars \(b_1,\ldots,b_k\text{,}\) not all equal to zero, such that \(b_1\uvec_1 + \cdots + b_k\uvec_k = 0.\)
    🔗
    Let \(j\) be the largest index such that \(b_j \neq 0\text{.}\) Then \(b_1\uvec_1 + \cdots + b_j\uvec_j = 0.\) Dividing by \(b_j\) and solving for \(\uvec_j\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \uvec_j = -\frac{b_1}{b_j}\uvec_1 - \cdots - \frac{b_{j-1}}{b_j}\uvec_{j-1}, \end{equation*}
    so \(\uvec_j\) can be written as a linear combination of earlier vectors as claimed.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.2.14.

    Consider a sequence of non-zero polynomials \(p_1(x),\ldots,p_k(x)\) of increasing degree, i.e., such that the degree of each \(p_i(x)\) is strictly larger than that of \(p_{i-1}(x)\text{.}\) We show that \(p_1(x),\ldots,p_k(x)\) are linearly independent in the vector space \(\mathbb{P}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    A polynomial of degree \(n\) cannot be a linear combination of polynomials of degree less than \(n\text{.}\) Therefore, none of the polynomials \(p_1(x),\ldots,p_k(x)\) can be written as a linear combination of earlier polynomials. By Proposition 5.2.13, \(p_1(x),\ldots,p_k(x)\) are linearly independent.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 5.2.15.

    Suppose \(\{\uvec_1,\ldots,\uvec_k\}\) is linearly independent and \(\vvec\notin \mathrm{span}\{\uvec_1,\ldots,\uvec_k\}\text{.}\) Then the set \(\{\uvec_1,\ldots,\uvec_k,\vvec\}\) is also linearly independent.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Assume, on the contrary, that the set were linearly dependent. Then by Proposition 5.2.13, one of the vectors can be written as a linear combination of earlier vectors. This vector cannot be one of the \(\uvec_i\text{,}\) because \(\uvec_1,\ldots,\uvec_k\) are linearly independent. It also cannot be \(\vvec\text{,}\) because \(\vvec\notin \mathrm{span}\{\uvec_1,\ldots,\uvec_k\}\text{.}\) Therefore, our assumption cannot be true, and the set is linearly independent.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 5.2.4 Summary

    • Linear combinations describe how new vectors can be formed by adding scalar multiples of given vectors, providing a systematic way to build vectors from other vectors.
      🔗
    • The span of a set of vectors consists of all vectors that can be formed as linear combinations of those vectors, allowing us to determine when a collection of vectors generates part or all of a vector space.
      🔗
    • Linear independence captures the idea of non‑redundancy: a set of vectors is linearly independent if none of its vectors can be written as a linear combination of the others.
      🔗
