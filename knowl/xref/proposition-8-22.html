Proposition 5.2.13.

Let \(V\) be a vector space, and let \(\uvec_1,\uvec_2,\ldots\) be a (finite or infinite) sequence of vectors in \(V\text{.}\) If \(\uvec_1,\uvec_2,\ldots\) are linearly dependent, then at least one of the vectors can be written as a linear combination of earlier vectors in the sequence:
\begin{equation*} \uvec_j = a_1\uvec_1 + a_2\uvec_2 + \cdots + a_{j-1}\uvec_{j-1}, \end{equation*}
for some \(j\text{.}\) We say that the vector \(\uvec_j\) is redundant.
🔗
🔗

Proof.
Suppose that the vectors are linearly dependent. Then the equation \(b_1\uvec_1 + \cdots + b_k\uvec_k = 0\) has a non-trivial solution for some \(k\text{.}\) In other words, there exist scalars \(b_1,\ldots,b_k\text{,}\) not all equal to zero, such that \(b_1\uvec_1 + \cdots + b_k\uvec_k = 0.\)
🔗
Let \(j\) be the largest index such that \(b_j \neq 0\text{.}\) Then \(b_1\uvec_1 + \cdots + b_j\uvec_j = 0.\) Dividing by \(b_j\) and solving for \(\uvec_j\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} \uvec_j = -\frac{b_1}{b_j}\uvec_1 - \cdots - \frac{b_{j-1}}{b_j}\uvec_{j-1}, \end{equation*}
so \(\uvec_j\) can be written as a linear combination of earlier vectors as claimed.
