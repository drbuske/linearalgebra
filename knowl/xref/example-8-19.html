Example 5.2.10.

Consider again the real-valued sequences from Example 5.2.5,
\begin{gather*} e^0=(1,0,0,0,0,\ldots),\\ e^1=(0,1,0,0,0,\ldots),\\ e^2=(0,0,1,0,0,\ldots), \end{gather*}
and so on. Let \(S=\{e^0,e^1,e^2,\ldots\}\text{.}\) This is an infinite subset of \(\mathrm{Seq}_{\mathbb{R}}\text{.}\) We show that \(S\) is linearly independent.
🔗
Since \(S\) is an infinite set, we have to show that every finite subset of \(S\) is linearly independent. So consider a finite subset
\begin{equation*} \{e^{k_1},e^{k_2},\ldots,e^{k_n}\}\subseteq S \end{equation*}
and assume that
\begin{equation*} a_1e^{k_1}+a_2e^{k_2}+\cdots+a_ne^{k_n}=0. \end{equation*}
🔗
We have to show that \(a_1,\ldots,a_n=0\text{.}\) Consider some index \(i\in\{1,\ldots,n\}\text{.}\) Then the \(k_i\)th element of \(a_1e^{k_1}+\cdots+a_ne^{k_n}\) is equal to \(a_i\) by the left-hand side of the equation, but it is also equal to 0 by the right-hand side. It follows that \(a_i=0\) for all \(i\in\{1,\ldots,n\}\text{.}\)
🔗
Therefore \(e^{k_1},e^{k_2},\ldots,e^{k_n}\) is linearly independent. Since this was an arbitrary finite subset of \(S\text{,}\) it follows, by definition, that \(S\) is linearly independent.
