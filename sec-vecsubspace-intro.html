    Section 5.3 Subspaces

    In this section we will consider subspaces of general vector spaces.
    🔗

    Preview Activity 5.3.1.

    In earlier sections, we studied vector spaces as entire collections of objects. In practice, however, we often work with smaller collections that live inside a larger vector space. In this activity, we explore when such a subset behaves like a vector space in its own right.
    🔗
    Rather than starting with a formal definition, you will examine examples and look for patterns that help explain which subsets work and which do not.
    🔗

    (a)

    Consider the following subsets of \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. All vectors of the form \(\begin{bmatrix}x \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. All vectors of the form \(\begin{bmatrix}x \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    3. All vectors of the form \(\begin{bmatrix}x \\ y \end{bmatrix}\) with \(x+y=0\text{.}\)
      🔗
    For each subset:
    🔗
    1. Decide whether adding two vectors from the subset produces another vector in the subset.
      🔗
    2. Decide whether multiplying a vector in the subset by a scalar keeps the result in the subset.
      🔗
    3. Identify one example where something fails, if it does.
      🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Now consider the following subsets of the vector space \(\mathbb{P}_2\) of polynomials of degree at most 2.
    🔗
    1. All polynomials with zero constant term.
      🔗
    2. All polynomials with constant term equal to 1.
      🔗
    3. All polynomials that have a fixed real number \(r\) as a root.
      🔗
    For each subset:
    🔗
    1. Decide whether the zero polynomial belongs to the subset.
      🔗
    2. Test whether the subset is closed under addition.
      🔗
    3. Test whether the subset is closed under scalar multiplication.
      🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    In each of the previous examples, some subsets behaved well under the vector space operations, while others failed in specific ways.
    🔗
    1. Which properties seem to fail most often?
      🔗
    2. Why does the failure of a single property prevent a subset from behaving like a vector space?
      🔗
    3. What role does the zero vector seem to play in all successful examples?
      🔗
    🔗
    These examples suggest that only certain subsets of a vector space are stable under the operations of addition and scalar multiplication. In the next section, we will formalize these observations by introducing the definition of a subspace.
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 5.3.1 Definition of subspace

    Definition 5.3.1.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space over a field \(K\text{.}\) A subset \(W \subseteq V\) is said to be a subspace of \(V\) if the following conditions hold:
    🔗
    1. \(\zerovec \in W\text{,}\) where \(\zerovec\) is the additive unit of \(V\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. \(W\) is closed under addition: Whenever \(\uvec,\vvec \in W\text{,}\) then \(\uvec+\vvec \in W\text{.}\)
      🔗
    3. \(W\) is closed under scalar multiplication: Whenever \(k \in K\) and \(\uvec \in W\text{,}\) then \(k\uvec \in W\text{.}\)
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.3.2.

    As we have seen, the subspaces of \(\mathbb{R}^3\) are:
    🔗
    • the zero subspace \(\{\zerovec\}\text{;}\)
      🔗
    • lines through the origin;
      🔗
    • planes through the origin;
      🔗
    • \(\mathbb{R}^3\) itself.
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.3.3.

    Let \(V=\mathrm{Func}_{\mathbb{R},\mathbb{R}}\text{,}\) the vector space of functions from real numbers to real numbers. Let \(W\subseteq V\) be the subset of continuous functions. Then \(W\) is a subspace of \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Proof: We know from calculus that:
    🔗
    1. the zero function, defined by \(f(x)=0\) for all \(x\text{,}\) is continuous;
      🔗
    2. if \(f,g\) are continuous functions, then \(f+g\) is continuous;
      🔗
    3. if \(f\) is a continuous function and \(k\) a constant, then \(kf\) is continuous.
      🔗
    It follows that \(W\) contains \(\zerovec\text{,}\) and is closed under addition and scalar multiplication. Therefore, \(W\) is a subspace of \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.3.4.

    Let \(V=\mathrm{Func}_{\mathbb{R},\mathbb{R}}\text{.}\) Recall from calculus that a function \(f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) is called differentiable if the derivative \(f'(x)\) exists for all \(x\in\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Let \(W\subseteq V\) be the subset of differentiable functions. Then \(W\) is a subspace of \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We know from calculus that:
    🔗
    1. the zero function, defined by \(f(x)=0\) for all \(x\text{,}\) is differentiable. In fact, its derivative is \(f'(x)=0\text{;}\)
      🔗
    2. if \(f,g\) are differentiable functions, then \(h=f+g\) is differentiable. In fact, \(h'(x)=f'(x)+g'(x)\text{;}\)
      🔗
    3. if \(f\) is a differentiable function and \(k\) a constant, then \(h=kf\) is differentiable. In fact, \(h'=kf'\text{.}\)
      🔗
    It follows that \(W\) contains \(\zerovec\text{,}\) and is closed under addition and scalar multiplication. Therefore, \(W\) is a subspace of \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.3.5.

    Let \(V=\mathrm{Seq}_{\mathbb{R}}\text{,}\) the vector space of sequences of real numbers. Let
    \begin{equation*} W=\{a\in\mathrm{Seq}_{\mathbb{R}}\mid \text{for all }n\ge 0,\ a_{n+2}=a_n+a_{n+1}\}. \end{equation*}
    In other words, \(W\) is the set of all sequences satisfying the recurrence relation \(a_{n+2}=a_n+a_{n+1}\text{.}\) Then \(W\) is a subspace of \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Proof: Before we prove that \(W\) is a subspace, let us first consider an example. The following sequences are elements of \(W\text{,}\) because they both satisfy the recurrence:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} a\amp =(1,1,2,3,5,8,13,21,\ldots),\\ b\amp =(1,3,4,7,11,18,29,47,\ldots). \end{align*}
    🔗
    Note that if we add these sequences, we get
    \begin{equation*} a+b=(2,4,6,10,16,26,42,68,\ldots), \end{equation*}
    which again satisfies the recurrence. Therefore, the set \(W\) is closed under the addition of these particular sequences \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We now prove the properties in general.
    🔗
    1. Let \(z\) be the zero sequence, defined by \(z_n=0\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Then \(z\) satisfies the recurrence relation, since for all \(n\ge 0\text{,}\) \(z_{n+2}=0=z_n+z_{n+1}\text{.}\) Therefore \(z\in W\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. To show that \(W\) is closed under addition, consider any two sequences \(a,b\in W\text{,}\) and let \(c=a+b\text{.}\) Then for all \(n\ge 0\text{,}\)
      \begin{equation*} c_{n+2}=a_{n+2}+b_{n+2} =(a_n+a_{n+1})+(b_n+b_{n+1}) =(a_n+b_n)+(a_{n+1}+b_{n+1}) =c_n+c_{n+1}, \end{equation*}
      so \(c\) satisfies the recurrence. It follows that \(c\in W\text{.}\)
      🔗
    3. To show that \(W\) is closed under scalar multiplication, consider any \(k\in\mathbb{R}\) and \(a\in W\text{,}\) and let \(c=ka\text{.}\) Then for all \(n\ge 0\text{,}\)
      \begin{equation*} c_{n+2}=ka_{n+2}=k(a_n+a_{n+1})=ka_n+ka_{n+1}=c_n+c_{n+1}, \end{equation*}
      so \(c\) satisfies the recurrence. It follows that \(c\in W\text{.}\)
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.3.6.

    Let \(V=\mathrm{Func}_{\mathbb{R},\mathbb{R}}\text{.}\) Recall from calculus that a differential equation is an equation about an unknown function and its derivatives. For example
    \begin{equation*} f''=-f \end{equation*}
    is a differential equation. The functions \(f(x)=\sin x\text{,}\) \(f(x)=\cos x\text{,}\) and \(f(x)=0\) are examples of solutions of this differential equation. Let \(W\) be the set of all functions that are solutions of the differential equation \(f''=-f\text{.}\) Then \(W\) is a subspace of \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Proof:The zero function \(f(x)=0\) is a solution of the differential equation, and therefore an element of \(W\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To show that \(W\) is closed under addition, let \(f,g\in W\) and consider \(h=f+g\text{.}\) Then \(f''=-f\) and \(g''=-g\text{,}\) and therefore \(h''=f''+g''=-f+(-g)=-h\text{.}\) Therefore \(h\in W\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To show that \(W\) is closed under scalar multiplication, let \(k\in\mathbb{R}\) and \(f\in W\text{,}\) and consider \(h=kf\text{.}\) Then \(f''=-f\text{,}\) and therefore \(h''=kf''=k(-f)=-h\text{.}\) It follows that \(h\in W\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.3.7.

    Consider \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{,}\) the vector space of polynomials of degree at most 2, with real coefficients. Fix some real number \(r\text{,}\) and let \(W\subseteq \mathbb{P}_2\) be the subset of polynomials that have \(r\) as a root. Then \(W\) is a subspace of \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Proof: We can express \(W\) as follows: \(W=\{p\in \mathbb{P}_2\mid p(r)=0\}.\) We need to show that \(W\) is a subspace.
    🔗
    \(1.\) The zero polynomial, given by \(0(x)=0\text{,}\) satisfies \(0(r)=0\text{,}\) so \(0\in W\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \(2.\) To show that \(W\) is closed under addition, assume \(p,q\in W\text{,}\) and let \(s=p+q\text{.}\) Then \(p(r)=0\) and \(q(r)=0\text{,}\) therefore \(s(r)=p(r)+q(r)=0\text{.}\) It follows that \(s\in W\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \(3.\) To show that \(W\) is closed under scalar multiplication, assume \(p(x)\in W\) and \(k\) be a scalar. Then \((kp)(r)=k(p(r))=k0=0\text{,}\) and therefore \(kp\in W\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.3.8.

    Let \(V\) be an arbitrary vector space over a field \(K\text{.}\) Then \(\{\zerovec\}\) is a subspace of \(V\text{,}\) called the zero subspace. Also, \(V\) is a subspace of itself.
    🔗
    Proof: Clearly \(\{\zerovec\}\) contains \(\zerovec\text{,}\) and is closed under addition and scalar multiplication because \(\zerovec+\zerovec=\zerovec\) and \(k\zerovec=\zerovec\) for all \(k\text{.}\) Similarly, \(V\) contains \(\zerovec\) and is closed under addition and scalar multiplication, because addition and scalar multiplication are operations on \(V\text{.}\) Therefore, both \(\{\zerovec\}\) and \(V\) are subspaces of \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 5.3.2 Subspaces are vector spaces

    The interest of subspaces lies in the fact that they are vector spaces in their own right, as stated in the following proposition.
    🔗

    Proposition 5.3.9.

    Let \(W\) be a subspace of a vector space \(V\text{.}\) Then \(W\) satisfies the vector space axioms (A1)–(A4) and (SM1)–(SM4), with respect to the same operations (addition and scalar multiplication) as those defined on \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Since \(W\) is a subspace, it is closed under addition and scalar multiplication. This ensures that addition and scalar multiplication are well-defined operations on \(W\text{.}\) The axioms (A1), (A2), (A4), and (SM1)–(SM4) all obviously hold in \(W\text{,}\) because they hold in \(V\) (two elements of \(W\) are equal in \(W\) if and only if they are equal in \(V\)). The axiom (A3) holds because \(\zerovec\in W\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 5.3.10.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space over some field \(K\text{,}\) and consider a set of vectors \(S\subseteq V\text{.}\) Then \(\mathrm{span}(S)\) is the smallest subspace of \(V\) containing \(S\text{.}\) More explicitly, we have:
    🔗
    1. The set \(\mathrm{span}(S)\) is a subspace of \(V\text{,}\) and \(S\subseteq\mathrm{span}(S)\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. If \(W\) is any other subspace of \(V\) such that \(S\subseteq W\text{,}\) then \(\mathrm{span}(S)\subseteq W\text{.}\)
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    1. To show that \(\mathrm{span}(S)\) is a subspace, first note that \(\zerovec\in\mathrm{span}(S)\text{,}\) because \(\zerovec\) is the empty linear combination. Also, if \(\vvec,\uvec\in\mathrm{span}(S)\text{,}\) then by definition of span, there exist \(\vvec_1,\ldots,\vvec_k,\uvec_1,\ldots,\uvec_\ell\in S\) and \(a_1,\ldots,a_k,b_1,\ldots,b_\ell\in K\) such that
      \begin{align*} \vvec \amp =a_1\vvec_1+\cdots+a_k\vvec_k,\\ \uvec \amp =b_1\uvec_1+\cdots+b_\ell \uvec_\ell. \end{align*}
      Then
      \begin{equation*} \vvec+\uvec=a_1\vvec_1+\cdots+a_k\vvec_k+b_1\uvec_1+\cdots+b_\ell \uvec_\ell, \end{equation*}
      and therefore \(\vvec+\uvec\in\mathrm{span}(S)\text{.}\) It follows that \(\mathrm{span}(S)\) is closed under addition. The proof for scalar multiplication is similar. Finally, every \(\vvec\in S\) is trivially a linear combination of itself, \(\vvec=1\vvec\text{,}\) and therefore \(S\subseteq\mathrm{span}(S)\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. Consider any other subspace \(W\) of \(V\) such that \(S\subseteq W\text{.}\) To show that \(\mathrm{span}(S)\subseteq W\text{,}\) consider an arbitrary element \(\vvec\in\mathrm{span}(S)\text{.}\) By definition of span, there exist \(\vvec_1,\ldots,\vvec_k\in S\) and \(a_1,\ldots,a_k\in K\) such that \(\vvec=a_1\vvec_1+\cdots+a_k\vvec_k.\) By assumption, \(\vvec_1,\ldots,\vvec_k\in W\text{.}\) Since \(W\) is closed under addition and scalar multiplication, it follows that \(\vvec\in W\text{.}\) Since \(\vvec\) was an arbitrary element of \(\mathrm{span}(S)\text{,}\) it follows that \(\mathrm{span}(S)\subseteq W\text{.}\)
      🔗
    🔗
    While the last proposition looks technical, it can actually be useful for proving that two sets of vectors span the same subspace. The following in an example of this.
    🔗

    Example 5.3.11.

    We show that the sets \(S=\{x^2-2x+1,\;x-1\}\) and \(T=\{x^2-1,\;x^2-x\}\) span the same subspace of \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{.}\) In other words, we show that
    \begin{equation*} \mathrm{span}(S)=\mathrm{span}(T). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To show that two sets are equal, we must show that each is a subset of the other. So we will show \(\mathrm{span}(S)\subseteq\mathrm{span}(T)\) and \(\mathrm{span}(T)\subseteq\mathrm{span}(S)\text{.}\) By Proposition 5.3.10, it is sufficient to show that \(S\subseteq\mathrm{span}(T)\) and \(T\subseteq\mathrm{span}(S)\text{,}\) i.e., we must show that every element of \(S\) is a linear combination of elements of \(T\) and vice versa.
    🔗
    \(1.\) \(S\subseteq\mathrm{span}(T)\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{align*} x^2-2x+1 \amp =(-1)(x^2-1)+2(x^2-x)\in\mathrm{span}(T),\\ x-1 \amp =1(x^2-1)-1(x^2-x)\in\mathrm{span}(T). \end{align*}
    Since each element of \(S\) is an element of \(\mathrm{span}(T)\text{,}\) it follows that \(S\subseteq\mathrm{span}(T)\text{.}\) By Proposition 5.3.10, this implies that \(\mathrm{span}(S)\subseteq\mathrm{span}(T)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    \(2.\) \(T\subseteq\mathrm{span}(S)\text{.}\) We have
    \begin{align*} x^2-1\amp =1(x^2-2x+1)-2(x-1)\in\mathrm{span}(S),\\ x^2-x\amp =1(x^2-2x+1)-1(x-1)\in\mathrm{span}(S). \end{align*}
    Since each element of \(T\) is an element of \(\mathrm{span}(S)\text{,}\) it follows that \(T\subseteq\mathrm{span}(S)\text{.}\) By Proposition 5.3.10, this implies that \(\mathrm{span}(T)\subseteq\mathrm{span}(S)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 5.3.3 Summary

    • A subspace is a subset of a vector space that contains the zero vector and is closed under addition and scalar multiplication.
      🔗
    • Many familiar collections, such as solution sets of linear equations, spaces of functions with special properties, and sets defined by linear conditions, naturally form subspaces.
      🔗
    • Every subspace is itself a vector space when equipped with the same operations as the larger space.
      🔗
    • The span of a set of vectors is the smallest subspace containing that set, providing a systematic way to describe subspaces.
      🔗

    Checkpoint 5.3.12. Identifying Vector Subspaces.

    • The set of all polynomials in \(\mathbb{P}_2\) of degree exactly \(2\text{.}\)
