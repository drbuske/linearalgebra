    Section 5.4 Basis and dimension

    As we did for \(\mathbb{R}^n\) earlier, we are also able to give meaning to basis and dimension for general vector spaces.
    🔗

    Preview Activity 5.4.1.

    In the previous sections, we studied spanning sets and linear independence. In this section, we combine these ideas to address a fundamental question: what is the smallest collection of vectors needed to describe every vector in a space?
    🔗
    Rather than starting with formal definitions, this activity asks you to experiment with representing vectors using different sets and to observe when representations are possible, unique, or redundant.
    🔗

    (a)

    Consider the vectors \(\uvec_1=\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) \(\uvec_2=\begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) and \(\uvec_3=\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Show that every vector in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) can be written as a combination of \(\uvec_1\) and \(\uvec_2\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. Show that every vector in \(\mathbb{R}^2\) can also be written as a combination of \(\uvec_1\) and \(\uvec_3\text{.}\)
      🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Now consider the set \(S=\left\{\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},\begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix},\begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\right\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Is it possible to remove one vector from \(S\) and still represent every vector in \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{?}\) Explain.
      🔗
    2. What does this suggest about whether all three vectors are necessary?
      🔗
    3. How might you recognize when a vector in a spanning set is unnecessary?
      🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Consider the vector space \(\mathbb{P}_2\) of polynomials of degree at most 2.
    🔗
    1. Explain why every polynomial in \(\mathbb{P}_2\) can be written using the set \(\{1,x,x^2\}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. Could a set with only two polynomials possibly describe every element of \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{?}\) Why or why not?
      🔗
    3. How does this compare to what you observed in \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{?}\)
      🔗
    🔗
    These examples suggest that certain spanning sets are minimal and free of redundancy, while others are not. In the remainder of this section, we will formalize these ideas using the concepts of basis and dimension.

    Subsection 5.4.1 Bases

    Definition 5.4.1.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space. A set \(\mathcal{B}\) of vectors is called a basis of \(V\) if
    🔗
    1. \(\mathcal{B}\) is a spanning set for \(V\text{,}\) and
      🔗
    2. \(\mathcal{B}\) is linearly independent.
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.4.2.

    Consider the vector space \(\mathbb{P}_2\) of polynomials of degree at most 2 with real coefficients.
    🔗
    • \(\{1,x,x^2\}\) is a basis of \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • \(\{x^2,(x+1)^2,(x+2)^2\}\) is a basis of \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • \(\{1,x-1,(x-1)^2\}\) is a basis of \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{.}\)
      🔗
    Unlike \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) a vector space like \(\mathbb{P}_2\) does not necessarily have a “standard” basis. One basis might be useful for one application, and another basis for a different application.
    🔗
    It is easy to verify that each set of vectors is linearly independent and spanning. See Example 5.2.7, Example 5.2.9, and Example 5.2.14 for similar calculations.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.4.3.

    Consider the vector space \(\mathbb{P}\) of all polynomials with rational coefficients. The following is a basis for \(\mathbb{P}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \{1,x,x^2,x^3,x^4,\ldots\}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Note that this basis is infinite.
    🔗
    Proof: The polynomials \(1,x,x^2,x^3,x^4,\ldots\) are linearly independent by Proposition 5.2.13. Namely, if they were linearly dependent, then one of the polynomials could be written as a linear combination of earlier ones. However, this is not possible because a polynomial of degree \(n\) cannot be a linear combination of polynomials of degree less than \(n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To show that the polynomials \(1,x,x^2,x^3,x^4,\ldots\) are a spanning set, consider an arbitrary element \(p(x)\) of \(\mathbb{P}\text{.}\) Then by definition, \(p(x)\) is of the form
    \begin{equation*} p(x)=a_nx^n+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots+a_1x+a_0, \end{equation*}
    for some \(n\ge 0\) and \(a_0,\ldots,a_n\in \mathbb{Q}\text{.}\) But then \(p(x)\) is a linear combination of \(1,\ldots,x^n\text{,}\) i.e., it is in the span of \(\{1,x,x^2,x^3,x^4,\ldots\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.4.4.

    Consider the vector space \(\mathrm{Seq}_{\mathbb{R}}\) of infinite sequences. As before, let \(e^k\) be the sequence whose \(k\)th element is 1 and that is 0 everywhere else, i.e.,
    \begin{align*} e^0\amp =(1,0,0,0,0,\ldots),\; \\ e^1\amp =(0,1,0,0,0,\ldots),\; \\ e^2\amp =(0,0,1,0,0,\ldots), \end{align*}
    and so on. Then the set
    \begin{equation*} \{e^0,e^1,e^2,\ldots\} \end{equation*}
    is not a basis of \(\mathrm{Seq}_{\mathbb{R}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Indeed, although we saw in Example 5.2.10 that the sequences \(e^0,e^1,e^2,\ldots\) are linearly independent, Example 5.2.5 shows that they are not spanning. Indeed,
    \begin{equation*} W=\mathrm{span}\{e^0,e^1,e^2,\ldots\} \end{equation*}
    is a subspace of \(\mathrm{Seq}_{\mathbb{R}}\text{,}\) consisting exactly of the finitely supported sequences, i.e., those sequences that have only finitely many non-zero components. Thus, \(\{e^0,e^1,e^2,\ldots\}\) is a basis of \(W\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The following theorem ensures that every vector space has a basis. We will not prove this theorem, because when the spaces are infinite-dimensional, the proof uses mathematics that is beyond the scope of this book. The proof uses a reasoning principle called the axiom of choice, which allows us to prove the existence of a basis even in cases where we cannot find an actual concrete example of a basis. For example, it is not possible to give a specific example of a basis for the space \(\mathrm{Seq}_{\mathbb{R}}\text{,}\) even though the following theorem guarantees that such a basis exists.
    🔗

    Theorem 5.4.5.

    Every vector space has a basis.
    🔗
    🔗
    It is useful to think about the Exchange Lemma in the context of \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) It is true in general vector spaces as well.
    🔗

    Lemma 5.4.6. [Exchange Lemma].

    Let \(V\) be a vector space over a field \(K\text{.}\) Suppose \(\uvec_1,\ldots,\uvec_r\) are linearly independent elements of \(\mathrm{span}\{\vvec_1,\ldots,\vvec_s\}\text{.}\) Then \(r\le s\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    An important consequence of the Exchange Lemma is that any two bases of a vector space have the same size.
    🔗

    Theorem 5.4.7.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space over some field \(K\text{,}\) and let \(\mathcal{B}_1\) and \(\mathcal{B}_2\) be bases of \(V\text{.}\) Then either \(\mathcal{B}_1\) and \(\mathcal{B}_2\) are both finite and have the same number of elements, or else \(\mathcal{B}_1\) and \(\mathcal{B}_2\) are both infinite.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    We first show that \(\mathcal{B}_1\) and \(\mathcal{B}_2\) are either both finite or both infinite. Assume one of them, say \(\mathcal{B}_1\text{,}\) is finite and contains \(s\) vectors. Since \(\mathcal{B}_1\) is spanning and \(\mathcal{B}_2\) is linearly independent, it follows from the Exchange Lemma that \(\mathcal{B}_2\) cannot contain more than \(s\) vectors, and in particular, \(\mathcal{B}_2\) must be finite. So the sets are either both finite or both infinite.
    🔗
    If they are both finite, say of size \(s\) and \(r\text{,}\) then by the Exchange Lemma, we have \(s\le r\) and \(r\le s\text{,}\) hence \(r=s\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 5.4.2 Dimension

    Theorem 5.4.7 allows us to define the dimension of a vector space.
    🔗

    Definition 5.4.8.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space over a field \(K\text{.}\) If \(V\) has a basis consisting of \(n\) vectors, we say that \(V\) has dimension \(n\text{,}\) and we write \(\dim(V)=n\text{.}\) In this case we also say that \(V\) is finite-dimensional. If \(V\) has an infinite basis, we say that \(V\) is infinite-dimensional, and we write \(\dim(V)=\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Note that the dimension is well-defined by Theorem 5.4.5 and Theorem 5.4.7, since these theorems ensure that every vector space has a basis (and therefore a dimension), and that any two bases are of the same size (and therefore a vector space cannot have more than one dimension).
    🔗
    We now calculate the dimensions of some vector spaces we encountered earlier.
    🔗
    • The space \(\mathbb{R}^n\) has dimension \(n\text{.}\)
      🔗
    • The space \(\mathbb{P}_2\) has dimension \(3\text{.}\) We found several bases for this space in Example 5.4.2.
      🔗
    • The space \(\Mvec_{m,n}(\mathbb{R})\) has dimension \(mn\text{.}\) A possible basis consists of all the matrices that contain a single \(1\) and zeros everywhere else.
      🔗
    • The space \(\mathrm{Func}_{X,K}\) is infinite-dimensional if \(X\) is an infinite set. If \(X\) is a finite set of \(n\) elements, then this space is \(n\)-dimensional. In that case, a basis is given by the set of functions whose value is \(1\) for one input and \(0\) for all other inputs.
      🔗
    • The space \(\mathrm{Seq}_{\mathbb{R}}\) is infinite-dimensional. We found an infinite linearly independent set in Example 5.2.10, showing that the space cannot be finite-dimensional.
      🔗
    • The space \(\mathbb{P}\) is infinite-dimensional. We found a basis for this space in Example 5.4.3.
      🔗
    • The subspace of \(\mathrm{Func}_{\mathbb{R},\mathbb{R}}\) consisting of the continuous functions is infinite-dimensional. For example, the functions \(1,x,x^2,x^3,\ldots\) form an infinite, linearly independent set of continuous functions.
      🔗
    • The subspace of \(\mathrm{Func}_{\mathbb{R},\mathbb{R}}\) consisting of the differentiable functions is infinite-dimensional. Again, the set \(1,x,x^2,x^3,\ldots\) is an infinite linearly independent set in this space.
      🔗

    Example 5.4.9.

    In Example 5.3.5, we considered the space \(W\) of sequences of real numbers that satisfy the recurrence \(a_{n+2}=a_n+a_{n+1}\text{.}\) What is the dimension of this space?
    🔗
    The space is \(2\)-dimensional. The easiest way to see this is to observe that a sequence \(a\in W\) is determined by its first two elements. We can say that the first two elements of the sequence are parameters, and all the other elements are then computed by the recurrence relation.
    🔗
    Specifically, suppose \(a_0=x\) and \(a_1=y\text{.}\) Using the recurrence relation to compute the remaining elements, we have
    \begin{align*} a\amp =(x,y,x+y,x+2y,2x+3y,3x+5y,\ldots) \\ \amp =x(1,0,1,1,2,3,\ldots)+y(0,1,1,2,3,5,\ldots). \end{align*}
    🔗
    Since this is the general form of the elements of \(W\text{,}\) and since the two sequences starting with \(1,0\) and \(0,1\) are clearly linearly independent, it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \{(1,0,1,1,2,3,\ldots),(0,1,1,2,3,5,\ldots)\} \end{equation*}
    is a basis of \(W\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.4.10.

    In Example 5.3.6, we considered the space of solutions of the differential equation \(f''=-f\text{.}\) What is the dimension of this space?
    🔗
    From calculus, we know that the general solution of the differential equation \(f''=-f\) is \(f(x)=A\sin x+B\cos x\text{,}\) where \(A,B\) are constants. We also know, from Example 5.2.12, that \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\) are linearly independent. It follows that \(\{\sin x,\cos x\}\) is a basis for the solution space. The solution space is therefore \(2\)-dimensional.
    🔗
    🔗
    We conclude this section by stating two properties of bases that generalize earlier theorems: every linearly independent set can be extended to a basis by adding 0 or more vectors, and every spanning set can be reduced to a basis by removing 0 or more vectors.
    🔗

    Proposition 5.4.11.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space, and let \(S\subseteq V\) be a linearly independent set of vectors. Then \(S\) can be extended to a basis of \(V\text{,}\) i.e., there exists a basis \(B\) of \(V\) such that \(S\subseteq B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 5.4.12.

    Let \(V\) be a vector space, and let \(S\subseteq V\) be a spanning set of \(V\text{.}\) Then \(S\) can be shrunk to a basis, i.e., there exists a basis \(\mathcal{B}\) of \(V\) such that \(\mathcal{B}\subseteq S\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.4.13.

    Let \(S\subseteq \Mvec_{2,2}(\mathbb{R})\) be the linearly independent set given by
    \begin{equation*} S=\left\{ \begin{bmatrix}1\amp0\\0\amp0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\amp1\\0\amp0\end{bmatrix} \right\}. \end{equation*}
    We enlarge \(S\) to a basis of \(M_{2,2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We can obtain a basis of \(\Mvec_{2,2}(\mathbb{R})\) by adding two more linearly independent matrices
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix}0\amp0\\1\amp0\end{bmatrix} \quad \textrm{ and } \quad \begin{bmatrix}0\amp0\\0\amp1\end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}
    The resulting basis is
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{B}=\left\{ \begin{bmatrix}1\amp0\\0\amp0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\amp1\\0\amp0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\amp0\\1\amp0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\amp0\\0\amp1\end{bmatrix} \right\}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.4.14.

    Consider the spanning set \(S\subseteq \mathbb{P}_2\) given by
    \begin{equation*} S=\{1,x,2x+1,x^2+1,x^2+2\}. \end{equation*}
    We shrink \(S\) to a basis of \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We use a version of the casting-out method. We examine each element of \(S\) from left to right and cast out the elements that are linear combinations of previous elements. Clearly the first two elements, \(1\) and \(x\text{,}\) are linearly independent.
    🔗
    The next element, \(2x+1\text{,}\) is redundant because it is a linear combination of \(1\) and \(x\text{.}\) The next element \(x^2+1\) is linearly independent of \(1\) and \(x\text{.}\) The final element \(x^2+2\) is redundant because it is a linear combination of \(1\) and \(x^2+1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Therefore, the following subset of \(S\) is a basis of \(\mathbb{P}_2\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{B}=\{1,x,x^2+1\}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Subsection 5.4.3 Summary

    • A basis of a vector space is a collection of vectors that is both spanning and linearly independent, allowing every vector in the space to be written uniquely as a linear combination of the basis vectors.
      🔗
    • Although a vector space may have many different bases, all bases of a given vector space have the same size, which captures an intrinsic property of the space.
      🔗
    • The dimension of a vector space is the number of vectors in any basis for the space, providing a way to measure the “size” or complexity of the space.
      🔗
    • Every vector space has a basis, and any linearly independent set can be extended to a basis, while any spanning set can be reduced to a basis.
      🔗
